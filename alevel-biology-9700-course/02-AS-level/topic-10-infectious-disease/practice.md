## Part A — 选择题 Multiple Choice（仿 Paper 1，每题 1 分）

**1.** Which organism is the vector for malaria?
- A. *Plasmodium falciparum*
- B. Female *Anopheles* mosquito
- C. Male *Anopheles* mosquito
- D. *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*

**2.** Penicillin kills bacteria by inhibiting the synthesis of cell walls. Why does penicillin not harm human cells?
- A. Human cells have cell walls made of cellulose, not peptidoglycan
- B. Human cells do not have cell walls
- C. Penicillin cannot enter human cells
- D. Human cells are too large for penicillin to affect

**3.** How is tuberculosis (TB) most commonly transmitted?
- A. Through contaminated water
- B. Via the bite of an infected mosquito
- C. Through inhalation of airborne droplets containing bacteria
- D. By direct skin contact with an infected person

**4.** Which statement about antibiotic resistance is correct?
- A. Individual bacteria mutate in response to being exposed to an antibiotic
- B. Antibiotics cause bacteria to evolve resistance through directed mutation
- C. Random mutations in bacteria can pre-exist before antibiotic exposure; selection then favours resistant variants
- D. Antibiotic resistance can only be acquired from other bacterial species

**5.** Which statement correctly describes HIV?
- A. HIV is a bacterium that can be treated with antibiotics
- B. HIV infects T helper cells and causes them to proliferate excessively
- C. HIV uses reverse transcriptase to produce DNA from its RNA genome
- D. HIV is transmitted only through blood transfusions

**6.** Which of the following is NOT a method to reduce the transmission of malaria?
- A. Using insecticide-treated bed nets
- B. Draining standing water to eliminate mosquito breeding sites
- C. Vaccination against the HIV virus
- D. Spraying insecticides in areas where mosquitoes breed

**7.** Which row correctly matches the pathogen type with the disease it causes?

| | Pathogen type | Disease |
|---|---|---|
| A | Virus | Tuberculosis |
| B | Protist | Malaria |
| C | Bacterium | Athlete's foot |
| D | Fungus | AIDS |

**8.** Why do antibiotics have no effect on viral infections?
- A. Viruses are too small to be reached by antibiotics
- B. Viruses do not have the cell structures (e.g. cell wall, 70S ribosomes) targeted by antibiotics
- C. Viruses mutate too quickly for antibiotics to work
- D. Antibiotics are broken down by viral enzymes

## Part B — 结构题 Structured Questions（仿 Paper 2）

**1.** (a) Define the term *infectious disease*. **[1]**

   (b) Name **two** types of pathogen and give **one** disease caused by each. **[4]**

   (c) Explain what is meant by the *vector* of a disease, using malaria as an example. **[2]**

**2.** (a) Describe how HIV is transmitted between humans. Give **two** routes. **[2]**

   (b) Explain how HIV causes the immune system to fail over time. **[3]**

   (c) A patient with AIDS develops pneumonia caused by a fungus that does not normally cause disease in healthy people. Explain why this patient is particularly susceptible. **[2]**

**3.** (a) Explain why streptomycin (an antibiotic that targets 70S ribosomes) can kill *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* but does not affect human cells. **[2]**

   (b) A patient is prescribed a 6-month course of antibiotics for TB. Explain why the full course must be completed, even if symptoms disappear after 2 months. **[3]**

   (c) Explain how antibiotic resistance can spread rapidly through a bacterial population. **[3]**

**4.** The incidence of TB is much higher in overcrowded, poorly-ventilated urban areas than in rural areas.
   (a) Explain why overcrowding increases the transmission of TB. **[2]**

   (b) Suggest **two** other social or environmental factors that could increase the incidence of infectious disease in a population. For each, explain the link to disease transmission. **[4]**

## Part C — 应用/数据题 Application（强化 AO2）

**1.** The graph shows the number of reported malaria cases per year in a country before and after a bed-net distribution programme began in Year 4.

*(Assume: Years 1–3: ~200,000 cases/year; Year 4: programme starts; Years 5–8: gradual decline to ~40,000 cases by Year 8.)*

   (a) Describe the trend in malaria cases shown in the graph, quoting relevant data. **[2]**

   (b) Explain how insecticide-treated bed nets reduce malaria transmission. **[2]**

   (c) Suggest **two** reasons why malaria cases did not fall to zero, even after widespread bed-net distribution. **[2]**

**2.** The table shows the percentage of *Staphylococcus aureus* isolates resistant to methicillin (an antibiotic) in different countries.

| Country | % resistant S. aureus (MRSA) |
|---|---|
| Country A | 2% |
| Country B | 45% |
| Country C | 28% |

   (a) Suggest **two** factors that might explain why Country B has a higher MRSA prevalence than Country A. **[2]**

   (b) Explain, using the concept of natural selection, how MRSA could have evolved in a hospital setting. **[4]**

   (c) Suggest why stopping the use of methicillin entirely might reduce MRSA prevalence over time. **[2]**
